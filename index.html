Cameras breaking stigmas

Many have heard the famous phrase: “A picture is more than a thousand words”, but what if instead of just one image, we put together several images to recreate a single one? Will it still transmit the same message to the viewer?

Eating disorders and their discrimination in society
  • Sofia Martinez

    “Since I was a teenager, I struggled with anorexia. The social pressure to be thin was overwhelming. I remember at school, my peers would call me 'skeleton' and make me feel like I was worthless. That discrimination not only affected my health, but also my self-esteem.”

  • Carlos Ruiz

    “For years I suffered from bulimia and, when I tried to seek help, people around me downplayed it. They said it was just a 'lack of willpower' problem. That lack of understanding and the stigma surrounding eating disorders made me feel even more isolated.”

  • José Hernández

    “Obesity has been a constant challenge in my life. For years, I faced teasing and disparaging looks from strangers and even family members. The discrimination I suffered because of my weight led me to develop an unhealthy relationship with food, and I'm still on the road to healing.”

  • Lucía Gómez

    “Despite having overcome anorexia, I still feel the after-effects of discrimination. Society glorifies certain bodies and looks down on others. Often, I find myself defending my experience and remembering that the struggle with mental health and eating disorders is real and deserves respect.”

Joining multiple small images together to form a larger, cohesive image is very appealing in society. Well, this type of digital art allows each individual photo to contribute to the totality of the work.
